Cleveland has a long history of removal operations along different timelines: geologic, civic, industrial, and emotional. The story read by residents throughout the history of this particular neighborhood is multi-faceted, crossing scales and timelines, involving actors like settlers, vandals, city departments, and corporations, all of this telling a tale of hope and progress that would ultimately yield to both unsanctioned loss and sanctioned removal.

For our site, removal has always been perceived as negative change. This “change” progressed to the point that even the inhabitants of the space experienced removal at the hands of foreclosure, another agent of loss. The C-Land 2015 Blight Survey offers unprecedented insights on citywide building conditions. Of the multiple survey letter grades assigned, buildings ranked “C” - tipping-point buildings whose future is precarious - provide the greatest opportunities to direct future change and question values associated with building removal. In order to leverage the opportunities offered by the identified “C” buildings, communities must accept a wider range of building removal agents. Architects and urban designers must also engage in the process of building removal and help envision the resultant conditions.

Could a neighborhood’s experience of “wholeness” be reversed such that wholeness is obtained through strategic removal? Through diversified and informed agency, blocks, then neighborhoods, cities could guide their own C-Change.

Completed as part of the Cleveland Urban Design Collaborative and with researcher Ryan Dewey.
